# CHAPTER ONE: **MOON**

<div class="chapter-meta">
2:33 AM IST, 2nd April, 2026
<br>
Bengaluru, India
</div>

***

The moon makes my Elsa poster glow.

I'm supposed to be sleeping but the light is too bright. It comes through the window and makes lines on my wall. The fan goes whirr-whirr-whirr and chops the lines up.

I get up. The floor is cold. I don't put on my slippers.

The window has bars Appa installed after the monkey came in and stole grapes from my plate. I press my face between two bars.

The moon is sitting on top of the mango tree. Perfect round. I can see the dark spots on it. Amma says those are mountains.

My tummy feels funny. Like when the lift goes down too fast.

There's something on the moon.

A spot. A new spot. Bigger than the other spots. And it's moving.

I blink hard. Three times. The spot is still there.

It gets bigger.

# CHAPTER TWO: DEVIATION

<div class="chapter-meta">
10:41 PM CEST, 1st April, 2026
<br>
Noordwijk, Netherlands
</div>

***

The lab is empty except for me and the hum of the servers.

I'm looking at the lunar mass spectrometer data. Third check tonight. Same result.

The numbers are wrong.

Not measurement error. Not calibration drift. Just... wrong.

Lunar reflectivity dropped 0.3% in the last six minutes.

I pull up the visual feed. Standard automated lunar tracking. The moon should be in the frame, upper right quadrant.

It is.

I zoom in.

There's a shadow that doesn't align with the terminator.

I check the timestamp. Real-time feed.

The shadow is growing?

I pull up the mass spec again. Reflectivity down another 0.2%. In two minutes.

My coffee is cold. I don't remember making it.

The shadow isn't spreading like atmosphere diffusion. It's... discrete.

I open the group chat. Forty-three members. Most asleep.

<div class="chat-log">

@evi: Anyone else seeing anomalous terminator shadow?

</div>

Three dots appear immediately. Dr. Okonkwo in Lagos.

<div class="chat-log">
@okonkwo: Yeah.. What is it?
</div>

Five more people come online.

Everyone's seeing it.

The visual feed updates. The shadow is bigger now. Definitely bigger.

A chunk of the moon is just... not there.

<div class="chat-log">
@evi: This is not possible.
</div>

# CHAPTER THREE: ORBITAL

<div class="chapter-meta">
10:03 PM UTC, 1st April, 2026
<br>
International Space Station, Low Earth Orbit
</div>

***

The coffee floats past my face in a perfect sphere.

I don't catch it.

Commander Volkov would yell at me for wasting drinking water, but Volkov is asleep.

Everyone is asleep except me and the hum of the life support systems.

I'm watching the moon through the Cupola window.

We orbit Earth sixteen times a day. I've seen the moon from space four hundred and twelve times.

This is different.

The terminator line - the shadow between lunar day and night - it's wrong. Too sharp. Too dark. Like something is eating the light.

I pull up the external camera feed on my tablet. Zoom to maximum.

There's a crater. Or what looks like a crater. But craters don't appear in thirty minutes.

I check the orientation data. We're approaching our night pass over the Pacific.

The moon will be fully visible in eight minutes.

My headset crackles. Houston is still awake.

*"ISS, this is Houston. Do you have visual on the lunar surface?"*

I key the mic. *"Da. I see it."*

*"Can you confirm the visual anomaly at coordinates..."*

*"I can confirm something is wrong with the moon."*

Silence.

*"ISS, we're going to need you to wake Commander Volkov."*

The coffee sphere hits the wall and splits into a thousand tiny pearls.

I keep watching.

The crater is growing.

# CHAPTER FOUR: BELIEF

<div class="chapter-meta">
11:33 PM IRST, 1st April, 2026
<br>
Qom, Iran
</div>

***

The prayer rug beneath my knees is worn smooth. My father's rug. His father's before that. The threads know the shape of our family's foreheads.

Through the window, the moon hangs over the courtyard fountain.

Something is wrong with God's creation.

My student Ahmad is at the door.

*"Ayatollah. You need to see the television."*

*"I am praying, Ahmad."*

*"Please, Ayatollah. The moon."*

The urgency in his voice makes me stop.

In the common room, fifteen of my students crowd around the small television. Al Jazeera. BBC. IRIB. All showing the same thing.

A wound in the face of the moon.

The announcer's voice is careful, professional, but I can hear the tremor underneath, *"..organizations worldwide are reporting what appears to be a mass displacement event on the lunar surface.."*.

Such clean words for wrongness.

I have studied the heavens for seven decades. I know every verse. *"He created the heavens and the earth for a purpose. He wraps the night around the day, and wraps the day around the night. And He has subjected the sun and the moon, each orbiting... for an appointed term."*

Ahmad's phone buzzes. Then another student's. Then all of them.

*"Ayatollah,"* Ahmad whispers. *"What does this mean?"*

I am seventy-three years old. I have answers for everything. The path of righteousness. The interpretation of law. The nature of faith. But I have no answer for this.

The students are all talking at once. Crying. Praying. Shouting.

My hands are shaking.

I look out the window.

The moon is smaller than it was five minutes ago.

# CHAPTER FIVE: DEPTH

<div class="chapter-meta">
5:03 AM CST, 2nd April, 2026
<br>
PLAN Submarine 412, South China Sea
68 meters below surface
</div>

***

Lieutenant Huang shakes his head, headphones pressed tight.

*"Nothing, Commander. Complete signal loss across all frequencies."*

Commander Wei checks the chronometer. 00:47. They're supposed to receive the hourly strategic brief in thirteen minutes. They haven't missed one in six months.

The submarine groans.

It's a sound Wei knows. Metal flexing. Normal operational stress.

Except it's not the normal sound.

*"Sonar, report."*

Junior Officer Liu looks up from his station, face pale in the green instrument glow.

*"Commander... the ocean is moving wrong."*

*"Explain."*

*"The pressure gradient. It's shifting. Like..."*

Liu's hands move helplessly over his controls.

*"Like the water doesn't know which way to go."*

Wei crosses to the sonar station.

The display shows their position: 68 meters, steady depth, trim balanced. But the external pressure reading is fluctuating. Point-two bar. Point-three. Point-one.

Water doesn't do that.

*"Check the sensors."*

*"I've checked them three times, Commander."*

The hull groans again. Longer this time. A deep metallic complaint that Wei feels in his sternum.

*"Surface traffic?"*

*"None detected. We're alone out here."*

Wei's been underwater for twenty-two days. He knows every sound, every vibration, every quirk of this steel tube. Something is wrong.

The submarine lurches.

Not violently. Not like a depth charge or collision. This is different. This is the ocean itself shifting, like the entire South China Sea just decided to slosh left.

Wei grabs the intercom. 

*"All hands, brace for.."*

Another lurch. Opposite direction.

# CHAPTER SIX: FALLING

<div class="chapter-meta">
5:03 PM BRT, 1st April, 2026
<br>
Javari Valley, Brazilian Amazon
Near the Peruvian border
</div>

***

The stars are moving.

I learned their paths from Grandfather. Every night, the same dance. The hunter. The serpent. The three sisters who carry water across the sky.

Tonight, they are falling.

Not all at once. One by one.

I am standing in the clearing where we prepare cassava. The village sleeps behind me. Even the night birds are quiet.

The first light appeared an hour ago. I thought it was a spirit. We see them sometimes, dancing between the trees. But this spirit began to fall.

Slowly. So slowly. Not like a shooting star that scratches across the black and disappears.

Now there are four.

Five.

Eleven.

They drift sideways, drawing lines of orange light that hang in the air long after they pass.

I should wake the elders. I should wake everyone. But my feet won't move. My tongue is heavy.

Another light appears. This one is different. Bigger. It tumbles as it falls, spinning like a seed pod in the wind.

The air smells wrong. Like the time lightning struck the old ceiba tree and the sap burned for three days.

I close my eyes.

The ground jumps.

When I open my eyes, there's a pillar of fire reaching up to where the stars should be.

# CHAPTER EIGHT: DARK

<div class="chapter-meta">
12:03 AM EEST, 2nd April, 2026
<br>
Spitalul Universitar de Urgență, București, Romania
</div>

***

*"Stay still, domnule Popescu,"* she murmurs. The old man's veins are like threads beneath paper.

The lights flicker again.

The backup lights kick in - those sickly yellow emergency bulbs that make everyone look dead already.

Then those go out too.

Complete darkness.

Mihaela freezes, needle half-inserted. She's been a nurse for nineteen years. She knows every centimeter of this ward in the dark.

*"Mihaela?"* The old man's voice is thin with fear.

*"It's fine, it's fine."* She's lying. The backup generators have never failed. Not once. Not even during the floods in '18.

The ward's window faces west. Normally she can see the lights of the city center, the illuminated cross on top of Patriarhiei, the red glow of the parliament building.

Nothing.

The old man grabs her wrist. His grip is surprisingly strong.

*"Sfârşitul,"* he whispers. *The end.*

*"Nu."* Mihaela pulls her hand free gently. *"Not yet, domnule Popescu. Not yet."*

# CHAPTER NINE: UNRAVELING

<div class="chapter-meta">
11:03 PM CAT, 1st April, 2026
<br>
Salonga National Park, DRC
Research Station Alpha-7
</div>

***

Outside, the shrieking gets louder.

I grab my field recorder and knife. Push through the tent flap.

The forest is moving.

Not wind. Not rain. The *forest* is moving.

A column of driver ants crosses the clearing. Must be ten million strong. They flow like black water, heading north. Driver ants don't migrate at night. Driver ants don't migrate at all.

Behind them, a bushbuck crashes through undergrowth. It sees me. Doesn't stop. Just keeps running north.

Then another. And another.

Something massive moves in the canopy. Running through the trees.

*North.*

Everything is running north.

The weaver birds' nests are empty. Three hundred nests I've monitored for five years.

All empty.

The birds are in the air, thousands of them, circling, circling.

A fruit bat hits the ground two meters from me.

Then another.

They're dropping from the sky.

I look up at the moon, it's.. almost gone.
